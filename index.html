A home training plan for severe fatty liver, built around my daily walk.

Walk daily. Lift quietly. Heal my liver.

The change that matters most is losing 7 to 10 percent of my body weight. My daily walk is the engine. These dumbbell sessions add the muscle that lowers liver fat even before the scale moves. Everything here is low impact, floor and standing only, and quiet enough for an apartment.

My NAFLD is severe. I need my doctor's clearance before the dumbbell work, plus baseline liver labs so I can track real progress, not just weight.

How much to lift, and when to add more

Starting weights for the dumbbell day. They are light on purpose while I learn the movements.

Where to start

These numbers are the total weight of each loaded dumbbell, bar included. For the first week or two they should feel easy. That is the point, while my form settles and my back learns the hinge. Load the plates to land close to each figure.

Goblet squat8 kg, one dumbbell
Romanian deadlift6 kg each hand
Dumbbell floor press5 kg each hand
Dumbbell row7 kg each hand
Overhead press4 kg each hand
Reverse lungebodyweight, then 4 kg each hand
Weighted glute bridge8 kg, one dumbbell
Farmer's carry8 kg each hand

When to add weight

One simple rule. Once I can finish every set at the top of the rep range, with clean form, two sessions in a row, I add the smallest jump. On a single dumbbell that is one 0.5 kg plate on each side, so 1 kg on the bar. On the two handed lifts, add that to each dumbbell. If my form breaks down at the new weight, I added too soon, so I take it back off. There is no rush.

How fast it climbs

Lower body and pulls move fastest at the start. Expect to add to the goblet squat, deadlift, row, glute bridge, and carry about every one to two weeks. The presses climb slower, more like every two to four weeks, in the same small 1 kg steps. On weeks the weight does not move, adding one rep or one set counts as progress too.

As a rough guide over three months, a beginner often takes the goblet squat from 8 kg toward the top of what one dumbbell holds, the deadlift from 6 to about 9 or 10 kg per hand, and the overhead press from 4 to about 7 kg per hand. One honest note about my set: each dumbbell maxes out near 10 kg, so the squat and deadlift will outgrow it within a couple of months. That is the moment to assemble the long barbell with the extension shafts, which takes every plate I own and carries those two lifts for a long time. My numbers will be my own, so the rep range and my form decide, not the calendar.

Stalls, and running out of plates

If a lift stalls for three sessions, or my low back feels tight, drop it by about 2 to 4 kg and build back up. That reset is part of the process, not a step back. When the big lifts run out of plates as dumbbells, since both hands share one set, build the barbell instead and load every plate on it, or add a set and lower each rep slowly to a count of three. That keeps them hard without needing more iron.
